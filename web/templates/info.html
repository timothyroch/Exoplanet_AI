KOI Variable Guide

← Back to Predictor

Each Kepler Object of Interest (KOI) feature helps the model decide whether an observed signal likely indicates a real exoplanet. Here’s what each variable means:

Orbital Period [days]

The time it takes for the planet to complete one orbit around its host star.

Transit Duration [hrs]

How long the planet takes to pass in front of the star, as seen from Earth.

Transit Depth [ppm]

The fractional decrease in starlight during the transit, measured in parts per million. Larger depths mean bigger planets or deeper transits.

Impact Parameter

Describes how centrally the planet crosses the star. A value of 0 means a perfect center-crossing, while values near 1 mean a grazing transit.

Transit Signal-to-Noise

Measures how clearly the transit stands out from background noise. Higher values mean a more confident detection.

Stellar Effective Temperature [K]

The temperature of the star’s surface. Hotter stars (higher K) emit more light and influence the transit’s brightness and depth.

Stellar Surface Gravity [log10(cm/s²)]

The gravitational acceleration at the star’s surface. Lower values indicate larger, puffier stars.

Kepler-band [mag]

The brightness of the star in Kepler’s observation band. Brighter stars have lower magnitude values.

Planet-Star Radius Ratio

The ratio of the planet’s radius to the star’s radius. It determines how much light the planet blocks during transit.

Stellar Metallicity [Fe/H]

Indicates how metal-rich the star is compared to the Sun. Higher metallicity often correlates with a higher chance of forming planets.

Data reference: NASA Exoplanet Archive
